Our Vision

Different approaches to ART

Spyridon Simotas

Spyridon Simotas is an Assistant Professor who teaches French language and culture at the University of Virginia. He believes in the transformative experiences of education as a path to self-discovery. Inspired by a series of artists who use personal data as creative material, he developed ART as an e-portfolio companion in an effort to encourage mindfulness, meta-cognition, and making in his classes.

Yitna

Yitna Firdyiwek is a Senior instructional Designer at A&S Learning Design & Technology and supports College faculty on the (re)design, development, and implementation of innovative approaches to technology integration in the undergraduate curriculum. Yitna’s academic background is instructional technology with a focus on composition and writing instruction. He has been researching ePortfolio pedagogy, which he sees as one of the most significant pedagogical innovations today. Yitna has designed experimental ePortfolio platforms for research purposes as well as collaborated in the integration of ePortfolios in a World Language Learning curriculum about which he has continued to publish with colleagues.

Justin

Justin Greenlee is a Visiting Assistant Professor in the art history department at Kenyon College and a lecturer at the University of Wisconsin-Green Bay. His research and teaching interests pertain to late medieval and early modern art in Italy, particularly objects that are created, acted on, and restored many times—works that frustrate a study of the moment of creation and involve analyses that move across time and geographic borders.

Justin joined the ART project in summer 2021, when he developed a set of question to help students notice more about the conditions of their learning environment. Prompts encourage micro-attentions and micro-reflections that engage with elements of focus and distraction, enthusiasm and apathy, energy and fatigue. Micro-attentions can be things a student notices about their body, their surroundings, and the interactions between them. Micro-reflections can be thing they notice in a slightly expanded frame, including what's on their mind, happening later today, and elsewhere.

He hopes to work with students in Fall 2021 to turn the data they collect throughout the semester into a compelling visualization of their learning journey: specifically, a Radar graph on a sticker, clothing patch, pin, or temporary tattoo. In his iteration, ART will be an easy, fun, and artistically creative project! For more information about Justin's research and teaching, visit justingreenlee.net.
